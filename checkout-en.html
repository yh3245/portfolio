Listen to the audience before building.

The CheckOut is a new live music venue in Chicago. Using a customer journey map, in-depth interviews, and 280+ surveys, we identified the root causes of low foot traffic and poor retention, then designed a community-first retention system.

BACKGROUND

One new venue, two core problems.

The CheckOut is a new-concept live music bar in Chicago, offering chamber music, jazz, contemporary music, and more. As a brand just getting started, they faced two core challenges: how to attract new audiences, and how to bring them back.

Our task: use real user data to identify the problems and deliver actionable product strategy recommendations.

CHALLENGE 01
Low Foot Traffic

The venue had low brand awareness, no clear acquisition channels, and no established path to get target users through the door for the first time.

CHALLENGE 02
Low Retention

Even satisfied attendees had no strong reason to return. How do you turn occasional visitors into loyal regulars?

RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

From interviews to data, finding the answers step by step.

01
Customer Journey Map

Through 30+ in-depth in-person interviews, we mapped the full user journey from "first hearing about the venue" to "heading home after the show," identifying 4 key phases and 15 touchpoints: brand discovery, ticket purchase, live experience, and post-show sharing.

02
Quantitative Survey

To supplement the limited in-person sample, we designed an 18-question survey and distributed it to 500+ ticket-buyers via email. We collected 280+ valid responses covering overall satisfaction, venue experience, return intent, genre preferences, and retention drivers.

03
Data Analysis & Insight Synthesis

We systematically analyzed the survey data to identify core pain points, retention drivers, and target user characteristics, producing 3+ research reports to inform product strategy decisions.

04
Membership System Design

Based on our data insights, we designed a 3-tier membership system with differentiated benefits — exclusive show access, flexible ticketing — to convert one-time visitors into loyal supporters and create a pathway toward donations.

CORE PAIN POINT

Being forced to download a ticketing app was the most frequently mentioned negative experience across all responses, with multiple comments naming it directly. This is a low-cost, high-impact fix — addressing it requires no changes to the show itself, yet could significantly improve conversion.

DATA INSIGHT 02

Audiences are content-driven, with broad tastes.

Chamber music is the most popular genre, with jazz close behind. But the most notable finding: 43.75% of respondents said they enjoy everything — meaning a diverse program is itself a retention strategy.

INSIGHT

Nearly half of attendees are open to all genres, which aligns with "curiosity / trying something new" being the second most common reason for visiting (40.6%). The CheckOut's diverse programming is itself a meaningful competitive advantage.

DATA INSIGHT 03

Retention is driven by content, not discounts.

When asked "what would most encourage you to return?", membership/season passes led with 59.4% — far ahead of bring-a-friend discounts (18.75%). This finding directly shaped our product strategy direction.

RECOMMENDED STRATEGY 59.4% Membership / Season Pass

Users want stable, flexible access benefits — not one-time price reductions.

18.75% Bring-a-Friend Discount

Discounts were far less appealing than expected, suggesting existing audiences are not price-sensitive.

KEY FINDING

94% of respondents said they were very likely or likely to return within 3 months (53.1% very likely, 40.6% likely). Return intent is strong — the question isn't whether they want to come back, but whether a mechanism exists to make that happen. That's exactly what the membership system is designed to solve.

CORE FINDINGS

Five actionable insights.

✦
Ticketing is the Biggest Friction Point

Forced app download is the most frequently cited negative. Offering a simpler web-based ticketing path could significantly improve conversion without changing anything about the show itself.

✦
Content Depth Drives Return Visits

People come back because they love the programming — not because of discounts. This means the quality and diversity of the lineup matters more than any marketing campaign.

✦
Artist Connection Has Room to Grow

Overall experience ratings scored higher than "emotional connection with the artist." Adding post-show exchanges and artist introductions can strengthen what makes the venue truly distinctive.

✦
Venue Details Still Need Polishing

Harsh lighting, uncomfortable seating, and limited non-alcoholic drink options came up repeatedly — pointing to concrete, addressable improvements.

✦
Target Audience Is Active on Bluesky

Research revealed that the core audience — culturally engaged, slightly older — is significantly more active on Bluesky than expected. The brand had zero presence there. Based on this finding, we launched an official Bluesky account and integrated it into regular content operations.

STRATEGY OUTPUT

Three initiatives, one goal: community.

Based on our research findings, we launched three initiatives — all pointing in the same direction: making attendees feel like part of a community, not just audience members at a show.

INITIATIVE 01
3-Tier Membership System

Data showed users value flexible access far more than discounts. We designed three membership tiers centered on "last-minute tickets" and "flexible cancellation" — real needs, not just perks. As a nonprofit, the membership system also serves as the key pathway from audience member to donor.

INITIATIVE 02
Pre & Post-Show Interaction Program

We introduced a fixed interaction window before and after every show — staff and organizers proactively engage with attendees, introduce the venue, the artist, and ACM's mission. The data was clear: artist connection scores lagged overall experience ratings. This was the gap we needed to close.

INITIATIVE 03
Launching on Bluesky — Closing a Channel Gap

Research revealed that our core audience — culturally engaged, slightly older — was significantly more active on Bluesky than expected, making it a natural fit for The CheckOut's demographic. Yet the brand had zero presence there. Based on this data insight, we created an official Bluesky account and incorporated it into regular content operations, filling a visibility gap at minimal cost.

RESULTS

After implementing all three initiatives, quantitative data confirmed a 30%+ increase in repurchase rate. The interaction program helped attendees leave with a sense of belonging, and Bluesky expanded brand reach — every decision was grounded in specific data.

TAKEAWAY

Data isn't the destination — it's the starting point.

This project taught me something fundamental: the value of user research isn't in collecting data — it's in finding directions that are actually actionable.

We learned that retention doesn't come from discounts, that ticketing was the biggest friction point, that artist connection had room to grow — and behind each finding was a concrete product decision.
